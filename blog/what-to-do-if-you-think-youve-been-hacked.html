What to Do If You Think You've Been Hacked

📅 16 Apr 2026·⏱ 7 min read· ✍ Emma Watts

Finding out one of your accounts has been hacked is a horrible feeling. But the most important thing is: don't panic. Act quickly and calmly, work through these steps in order, and you can get things back under control.

Signs Your Account May Have Been Hacked

  • You receive a "login from a new device" alert you don't recognise
  • Friends tell you they got a strange message from your account
  • You find emails or messages you didn't send
  • Your password suddenly doesn't work
  • You notice purchases or transactions you didn't make
  • You receive password reset emails you didn't request

Step 1: Change Your Password Immediately

If you can still log in, change your password right now. Use our free password generator to create a strong, unique replacement — 20 characters, all character types. Don't use anything similar to the old password.

If you can't log in because the attacker changed your password, use the "Forgot password" or "Reset password" option on the login page.

Step 2: Enable Two-Factor Authentication

Once you're back in, turn on two-factor authentication straight away if it isn't already on. Even if someone gets your password again, they still won't be able to log in without your phone. See our guide on how to set up two-factor authentication simply.

Step 3: Check for Suspicious Activity

Look through the account for anything the attacker might have done:

  • Email: Check "Sent" for any emails they sent pretending to be you. Check mail rules or filters — attackers sometimes set up forwarding rules to keep seeing your emails. Check your linked phone numbers and backup email addresses to see if they changed them.
  • Social media: Check posts, messages, and apps that are connected to your account. Revoke access for any apps you don't recognise.
  • Shopping accounts: Check order history for any purchases you didn't make. Contact the retailer immediately if you find any.

Step 4: Change the Same Password Everywhere Else

If you used the same password on other websites — and according to research, 65% of people do — change it on all of them. Attackers know this and will try your stolen password on other sites automatically. Start with your email, banking, and any site with payment card details saved.

Step 5: Tell Your Bank If Any Money Has Been Taken

If any money has disappeared from a bank account or PayPal, contact your bank immediately. Tell them your account was compromised. They have fraud teams and can often reverse unauthorised transactions, especially if you report quickly. In the UK, call the number on the back of your card or use your banking app's fraud report feature.

Step 6: Check HaveIBeenPwned

Go to haveibeenpwned.com and enter your email address. This shows you if your details have appeared in any known data breaches — helping you understand which accounts might have been at risk and need their passwords changed.

Step 7: Set Up Alerts

Sign up for free breach alerts at HaveIBeenPwned so you're notified immediately if your email appears in future breaches. Enable login alerts on your most important accounts too — most email providers and banks offer these under security settings.
