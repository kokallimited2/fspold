Why You Need a Different Password for Every Website

📅 4 May 2026·⏱ 5 min read· ✍ Emma Watts

Most of us know we should use different passwords for different websites. Most of us don't. According to research by LastPass, 65% of people use the same password across multiple accounts. And that one habit puts everything at risk.

Here's What Actually Happens When a Website Gets Hacked

Imagine you have an account on a small online shopping site — a candle shop, say. One day, that shop's database is hacked and your email address and password are stolen. You probably won't even hear about it for months.

Meanwhile, attackers are selling that stolen list to other criminals. Someone buys it and feeds it into an automated program — a bot — that tries every email and password combination on hundreds of popular websites: Gmail, Amazon, PayPal, Netflix, bank websites.

If you used the same password on that candle shop as your Gmail account, the bot will get in. And once they're in your email, they have the keys to everything — because password reset emails go to your inbox.

This automated attack is called credential stuffing. It costs almost nothing to run and succeeds millions of times every day.

How Many Websites Have Been Hacked?

More than you'd think. HaveIBeenPwned — a free service run by respected security researcher Troy Hunt — catalogues known data breaches. As of 2026, it lists:

  • Over 700 confirmed breaches from named companies
  • More than 14 billion stolen credentials
  • Breaches including LinkedIn, Adobe, Yahoo, Dropbox, and hundreds of smaller sites

And those are just the ones that have been publicly disclosed. Many breaches are never reported.

The Simple Fix: One Site, One Password

The solution is straightforward — but it sounds impossible until you discover password managers:

  1. Every website gets its own unique, randomly generated password
  2. A password manager stores and fills them automatically
  3. You only need to remember your master password

For everything you need to know about password managers — including free options that work brilliantly — see our guide on the best free password managers for beginners in 2026.

Where to Start Right Now

If changing all your passwords at once feels overwhelming, start with just three accounts — these are the ones that cause the most damage if compromised:

  1. Your email account — this is the master key to everything. Password reset links go here.
  2. Your bank or PayPal — direct financial risk.
  3. Your most-used shopping site — often has saved payment cards.

Use our free password generator to create a unique password for each, and save them in a password manager. Then work through your other accounts at your own pace.

How to Check If Your Passwords Have Already Been Stolen

Go to haveibeenpwned.com and enter your email address. It's free, safe, and trusted by security professionals worldwide. It will show you every known breach that has included your email address — so you know exactly which accounts to change first.
