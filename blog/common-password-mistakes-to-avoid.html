7 Common Password Mistakes (And How to Fix Them)

📅 9 Apr 2026·⏱ 6 min read· ✍ Emma Watts

Even people who care about their online security often make these mistakes. The good news: once you know about them, they're easy to fix. Let's go through the most common ones.

Mistake 1: Using the Same Password on Multiple Sites

This is the most dangerous of all. When one website gets hacked — and it will eventually — attackers immediately test your password on hundreds of other sites. Your email, shopping, banking, and social media accounts all become vulnerable at once.

Fix it: Use a unique password for every site. A password manager makes this effortless. See our guide on the best free password managers for beginners.

Mistake 2: Using Personal Information

Your pet's name, your birthday, your children's names, your football team — these feel personal and memorable, which is exactly why they're dangerous. Attackers research their targets using social media, and these are the first things they try.

Fix it: Use randomly generated passwords with no personal connection. Our free generator creates completely random passwords that attackers can't guess.

Mistake 3: Short Passwords

Passwords like "Fluffy!" or "London24" might feel secure because they have capital letters and special characters. But at only 7-8 characters, automated cracking tools can work through the possibilities in seconds or minutes.

Fix it: Always use 16 characters or more. Security experts at NIST recommend at least 15 characters as of 2025.

Mistake 4: Predictable Substitutions

Using @ instead of a, or 0 instead of o, or 3 instead of e — these substitutions are so well-known that they're built into every password-cracking program. "P@ssw0rd" is just as easy to crack as "Password."

Fix it: Don't rely on substitutions to make a weak password stronger. Generate a genuinely random password instead.

Mistake 5: Seasonal or Year-Based Passwords

Passwords like "Summer2026!", "Spring2025#", or "January2026" are incredibly common — and incredibly easy to guess. Attackers run dictionaries containing every month, season, and year combination imaginable.

Fix it: Never base a password on the current date, season, or year. If you're changing a password, don't just update the year.

Mistake 6: Writing Passwords Where Others Can See Them

A sticky note on your monitor is like leaving your house key in the door. Anyone who can see your screen — in a café, at work, or even on a video call — can see it too.

Fix it: Use a password manager to store passwords digitally with encryption. If you must write a password down, keep it in a locked drawer or a physical safe — not stuck to your screen.

Mistake 7: No Two-Factor Authentication

Even a perfect password can be stolen — through phishing, malware, or a data breach at a website you use. Two-factor authentication means that even with your password, an attacker still can't get in without your phone.

Fix it: Turn on two-factor authentication for your email account first, then your bank, then your social media. Our guide explains how to set up two-factor authentication in simple steps.

The Quickest Win: Fix the Top Three

If you can only do three things today:

  1. Change your email password to a unique, 20-character randomly generated password
  2. Turn on two-factor authentication for your email
  3. Install a free password manager (Bitwarden is excellent and free)

Those three steps will protect you from the vast majority of attacks. You can gradually improve your other accounts over the coming weeks.
