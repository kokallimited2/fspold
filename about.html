Our Mission

Free Strong Password exists to make online security genuinely accessible to everyone — not just people who understand technology. Most security advice is written for experts. We write for your mum, your neighbour, and your colleague who just wants to stay safe online.

We believe that strong password habits shouldn't require a computer science degree. Our beginner-friendly wizard, plain-English guides, and jargon-free explanations are designed for the 85% of internet users who don't consider themselves "tech people" — but still deserve to be safe online.

About the Author

Emma Watts is a Digital Safety Writer who has spent years translating cybersecurity guidance into language that everyday internet users can actually understand and act on. Emma's writing focuses on the practical steps real people can take — not theoretical frameworks or enterprise-level policies.

Before specialising in digital safety writing, Emma worked in consumer education and public information. That background shapes everything on this site: content is written to be understood on first reading, without requiring any prior knowledge.

Areas of focus

  • Password security in plain English — length, randomness, and what actually matters
  • Two-factor authentication — what it is and how to set it up simply
  • Password managers — helping beginners choose and start using one
  • What to do after a hack — calm, step-by-step recovery guidance
  • Online privacy — practical steps for everyday users

All technical advice on this site is based on guidance from the UK's National Cyber Security Centre (NCSC), NIST SP 800-63B, and security researchers including Troy Hunt.

Editorial Standards

We only publish advice that we can back up with a source — specifically from NCSC, NIST, Verizon DBIR, or IBM Cost of Data Breach. When security guidance changes — as NIST's did in July 2025 — we update our content to reflect it.

Affiliate relationships are disclosed on the Affiliate Disclosure page and individually next to each link. We recommend tools because they're genuinely good for beginners — not because of commission rates.
